\begin{abstract}
Deep reinforcement-learning market makers are often trained with hybrid rewards that mix dampened profit-and-loss, transaction advantage, and inventory penalties. We revisit the Attn-LOB / C-PPO market maker of \citet{guo2023mm} and ask whether the behavioural prior can instead come from a classical quoting model. We propose \softas: PPO with pure profit as the economic reward and a soft action-space regularizer toward a per-stock Avellaneda--Stoikov (AS) teacher fitted on training data. On a synthetic, paper-shaped replication benchmark, the selected soft-AS policy improves mean episode PnL over reward-shaped \cppo{} by $15.10$ across 15 stock--seed pairs ($95\%$ CI $[9.19,21.01]$, $p=8.1\times10^{-5}$) and over \profitppo{} by $9.50$ ($95\%$ CI $[5.45,13.56]$, $p=1.9\times10^{-4}$). It also stays much closer to the AS teacher in action space. The gain is not free: \softas{} quotes tighter, fills more, and carries more inventory, especially under high latency. The result is therefore a focused claim: AS regularization is a cleaner, interpretable way to steer a deep market maker than hybrid reward shaping in this controlled benchmark.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Market making is the problem of continuously quoting bid and ask limit orders to earn the spread while managing inventory risk \citep{stoll1989,odhara1998}. A modern limit order book (LOB) is high-dimensional, noisy, and fast-moving, so recent work uses deep reinforcement learning (RL) to learn quoting policies directly from LOB state. The Attn-LOB / C-PPO agent of \citet{guo2023mm} is a strong example: a convolutional-attention encoder reads a rolling LOB window, and PPO emits continuous bid/ask quote parameters.

The agent's behaviour, however, is largely determined by a hand-designed reward. The original C-PPO reward combines dampened PnL, trading PnL, and an inventory penalty:
\begin{equation}
R_t
= \underbrace{\Delta\mathrm{PnL}_t-\max(0,\eta\Delta\mathrm{PnL}_t)}_{\text{dampened PnL}}
+ \underbrace{X_{t,v}(p_{m,t}-X_{t,p})}_{\text{trading PnL}}
- \underbrace{\zeta \mathrm{Inv}_t^2}_{\text{inventory penalty}} .
\label{eq:hybrid}
\end{equation}
These terms are useful engineering, but they are not potential-based reward shaping \citep{ng1999}; they change the optimization target and introduce coefficients that must be retuned for each market.

We replace this hybrid reward with a simpler separation. The economic objective is pure profit. The behavioural prior is supplied by Avellaneda--Stoikov (AS), a classical market-making model that gives an interpretable reservation price and spread from inventory, volatility, time remaining, risk aversion, and liquidity \citep{avellaneda2008}. Instead of copying AS, we regularize the learned continuous action toward a fitted AS teacher:
\begin{equation}
J(\pi)
= \E_{\pi}\!\left[\sum_t \Delta\mathrm{PnL}_t
- \lambda \left\lVert a^\pi_t-a^{\mathrm{AS}}_t \right\rVert_2^2 \right].
\label{eq:softas}
\end{equation}
This is not an unconstrained profit-only agent; it is profit-based RL with an explicit, inspectable policy prior.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, we formulate AS guidance as a soft action-space regularizer for continuous-action PPO market making. Second, we calibrate one AS teacher per stock from the training window and keep the extension code separated from the paper-replication pipeline. Third, we run a matched synthetic benchmark following the original paper's stock/day/episode/action structure. The main comparison uses five PPO seeds for \softas{}, \cppo{}, and \profitppo{} on three synthetic stocks. Fourth, we regenerate the original-style analyses--performance by stock, latency, attention, and decision traces--for the proposed method, and add teacher-action divergence diagnostics.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

Classical market-making models derive quotes by balancing spread capture and inventory risk. In the AS model, with inventory $q$, mid-price $s$, volatility $\sigma$, risk aversion $\gamma$, liquidity $\kappa$, and horizon $T$, the reservation price and total spread are
\begin{equation}
r(s,q,t)=s-q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t),
\qquad
\delta^a+\delta^b=\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)+\frac{2}{\gamma}\log\!\left(1+\frac{\gamma}{\kappa}\right).
\label{eq:as}
\end{equation}
This structure is interpretable but depends on assumptions about price and arrival processes \citep{gueant2013}. Deep LOB models such as DeepLOB and related architectures learn representations directly from order-book tensors \citep{zhang2019deeplob,tsantekidis2020,ntakaris2019}. RL market makers have used handcrafted state, discrete actions, and model-free policies \citep{lim2018,spooner2018,zhong2020,sadighian2019,gasperov2021}. \citet{guo2023mm} combine an Attn-LOB encoder with a continuous, AS-like action space and the hybrid reward in Eq.~\eqref{eq:hybrid}. We keep their encoder, simulator, action space, and PPO training setup, but move the behavioural structure from the hybrid reward into an AS action prior.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Replicated agent.}
The state is a rolling $50\times40$ tensor of the top-10 ask/bid prices and volumes, plus agent state such as inventory and remaining episode time. The Attn-LOB feature extractor applies convolutional filters, an Inception block, and multi-head self-attention. The continuous policy emits two normalized actions, $A_1,A_2\in[0,1]$: a reservation-price bias and a quoted spread,
\begin{equation}
\delta = A_1\cdot\text{max\_bias},\quad
p_r=p_m-\mathrm{sign}(\mathrm{Inv})\delta,\quad
\mathrm{spread}=A_2\cdot\text{max\_spread},\quad
p_{a,b}=p_r\pm \frac{\mathrm{spread}}{2}.
\label{eq:action}
\end{equation}
We retain the paper's $\text{max\_bias}=0.05$, $\text{max\_spread}=0.1$, $\text{minimum\_trade\_unit}=100$, and end-of-episode liquidation.

\paragraph{AS teacher.}
At each step we convert Eq.~\eqref{eq:as} into the same normalized action coordinates as Eq.~\eqref{eq:action}:
\begin{equation}
a^{\mathrm{AS}}_t
= \left(
\mathrm{clip}\!\left(\frac{|q|\gamma\hat{\sigma}^2(T-t)}{\text{max\_bias}},0,1\right),
\mathrm{clip}\!\left(\frac{\delta^a+\delta^b}{\text{max\_spread}},0,1\right)
\right).
\label{eq:teacher}
\end{equation}
The teacher is deterministic once $(\gamma,\kappa)$ are fitted. We estimate one teacher per stock from the training data: $\kappa$ from the mean level-1 half-spread and $\gamma$ from realized volatility, then use a lower-risk $\gamma=0.025$ for the selected policy. The resulting $\kappa$ values are $125.2$, $23.9$, and $83.8$ for stocks 000001, 000858, and 002415.

\paragraph{Methods compared.}
The main paper reports three PPO methods. \cppo{} is the reward-shaped baseline of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hybrid}. \profitppo{} removes the hybrid reward and trains on pure profit only. \softas{} trains on the profit objective plus the AS action regularizer in Eq.~\eqref{eq:softas}, with $\lambda=0.10$ and the low-risk per-stock teacher. Hard AS constraints, BC warm start, direct AS, and $\lambda$ sensitivity are appendix-only controls.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

The original Shenzhen Stock Exchange LOB data are proprietary. We therefore use a synthetic event-level LOB generator that preserves the experimental shape of \citet{guo2023mm}: three stock identities, top-10 book levels, stable intraday windows, 10 train days, 11 test days, and 2000-event episodes. The simulator replays the synthetic stream and fills the agent's quotes only when the recorded trade stream crosses the quote. There is no market impact and no transaction cost. This setup is appropriate for method comparison and debugging; it does not support live-market performance claims.

The original code trains one model per stock, not a single shared cross-stock policy, and we keep that design. The main comparison uses five PPO seeds per stock for each of \cppo{}, \profitppo{}, and \softas{}: 15 paired stock--seed units per method. All learned methods use the same state, encoder, action space, trade granularity, train/test split, and 400k PPO training steps.

For an episode with final marked-to-market value $V_T$ and initial value $V_0$, $\mathrm{PnL}=V_T-V_0$. We define $\mathrm{ND\mbox{-}PnL}=\mathrm{PnL}/(\overline{s}+10^{-7})$, where $\overline{s}$ is the mean quoted spread in the episode. Sharpe is computed on event-to-event marked-value increments $\Delta V_t$ as $\sqrt{n}\,\overline{\Delta V}/\mathrm{sd}(\Delta V)$, with no annualization. Paired tests use stock--seed units, not individual episodes.

Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figure~\ref{fig:overview} give the main result. \softas{} is best on mean PnL, ND-PnL, and Sharpe. The profit-only control is also stronger than the original reward-shaped baseline, so part of the improvement comes from removing the old hybrid reward. \subsection{The AS prior changes behaviour directly}

The main advantage of AS regularization over reward shaping is inspectability: at evaluation time we can log both the policy action and the AS teacher action. Figure~\ref{fig:teacher} shows that \softas{} is substantially closer to the teacher in both quote-bias and spread coordinates. The mean L2 action gap falls from $0.468$ for \cppo{} and $0.469$ for \profitppo{} to $0.230$ for \softas{}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig_teacher_divergence.png}
\caption{Teacher-divergence diagnostics from saved seed-0/1/2 checkpoints. \softas{} remains much closer to the fitted AS teacher than either control, showing that the regularizer directly steers quoting behaviour rather than only changing terminal PnL.}

This alignment is not free. The same diagnostic run shows more fills per episode for \softas{} ($96.8$ versus $54.4$ for \cppo{} at the logged evaluation granularity) and larger inventory excursions. In the five-seed training evaluation, \softas{} also has higher average inventory and more fills than both controls (Table~\ref{tab:main}). The correct conclusion is not that AS regularization dominates every risk axis; it gives a transparent knob for moving the policy toward an interpretable market-making regime.

\subsection{Latency remains the main weakness}

The original paper studied latency, so we reran an evaluation-only latency sweep for the saved seed-0/1/2 checkpoints at 1, 5, 10, 20, and 50 events. Figure~\ref{fig:latency} shows the key caveat. At low latency, \softas{} has the best return. As latency grows, all methods degrade, and the more active \softas{} policy degrades faster because stale quotes and larger inventory become costly. This is a useful stress test for the paper: it supports the low-latency result and exposes where an AS-regularized market maker needs stronger inventory or latency-aware controls.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why soft regularization helps.}
The three main methods separate the effect of the old reward from the effect of the AS prior. \profitppo{} beats \cppo{}, meaning the hybrid reward was not ideal in this synthetic setting. \softas{} then beats \profitppo{}, meaning the classical teacher adds useful structure beyond merely removing reward shaping. Hard AS constraints, reported in Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}, were weaker: clipping the policy near AS removes profitable deviations. The soft prior is useful precisely because it is defeasible.

\paragraph{What the result does and does not claim.}
The claim is intentionally narrow. On a paper-shaped synthetic benchmark, with per-stock policies and matched seeds, AS-regularized PPO is a stronger and more interpretable alternative to hybrid reward shaping. It is not a claim of universal superiority. The method trades more and carries more inventory. It is also latency-sensitive. A live or proprietary-data claim would require a real event stream, transaction costs, market impact, and stricter tail-risk constraints.

\paragraph{Why this is still useful.}
Reward shaping hides design decisions inside scalar coefficients. AS regularization exposes them: the teacher can be inspected, calibrated, logged, and stress-tested. If the policy deviates from AS, that deviation is measurable. If the teacher is too aggressive, $\gamma$, $\kappa$, or $\lambda$ can be changed directly. That makes the approach easier to reason about than a hybrid reward whose terms interact indirectly.

\section{Conclusion}

We revisited a reward-shaped deep market maker and replaced its hybrid reward with a profit objective plus a soft AS action prior. On a controlled synthetic replication of the original paper's experimental setup, the selected \softas{} policy beats both reward-shaped \cppo{} and a profit-only PPO control over five seeds per stock. The new diagnostics also sharpen the paper's limitations: the method is more active, more inventory-bearing, and more fragile under high latency. The strongest defensible conclusion is therefore not that AS regularization is risk-free, but that it is a cleaner and more interpretable way to steer deep market-making behaviour than hand-crafted hybrid reward shaping.

\section{Synthetic benchmark}
\label{app:data}

The synthetic generator produces event-level LOB days with a latent fair-value process, bid--ask bounce, stable/trending/volatile regimes, market-order flow, and top-10 price/volume levels. Each day has $10{,}000$ raw events before stable-window filtering; downstream evaluation keeps the original paper's stable trading windows, 10:00--11:30 and 13:00--14:30. The three instruments use base prices $16.45$ (000001), $130.00$ (000858), and $35.00$ (002415). The replay simulator has no market impact and no transaction cost, so these experiments are controlled method comparisons rather than market-deployment evidence.
